# Step 4: Mine Expired Patents

Patents expire after 20 years. Everything in them enters the public domain. Most of it was never successfully commercialized. This is the least-crowded part of the idea space — and the part most systematically ignored.

---

## Prompt Template

```
My friction point: [PASTE OUTPUT FROM STEP 1]
My domain: [DOMAIN]

I want to find expired patents — filed more than 20 years ago — that attempted
to solve this problem or a structurally similar one.

Search by mechanism, not by product name. For each candidate:
1. Plain-language description of the mechanism
2. Original application domain
3. Why it likely failed or stalled commercially at the time
4. What has changed since filing — materials, regulation, computing costs,
   manufacturing, market familiarity — that changes the viability calculus
5. Relevance to my friction point: High / Medium / Low

Return 4–6 candidates.
```

---

## Why Old Ideas Are Worth Searching

An expired patent may be newly viable because of changes in four categories:

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Technical readiness** | The mechanism required components that now exist: cheaper sensors, better batteries, additive manufacturing, mature ML |
| **Economic readiness** | Manufacturing costs have fallen; what required factory-scale can now be done at bench scale |
| **Market readiness** | Customers now recognize the problem category; regulatory frameworks now exist |
| **Combinatorial readiness** | The real value required pairing with something else that did not yet exist |

## Where to Search Yourself

| Tool | Best For |
|------|----------|
| [Google Patents](https://patents.google.com) | Full text search, expiration status, citation graph |
| [Lens.org](https://www.lens.org) | Patent families, citation analysis, open access |
| [Espacenet](https://worldwide.espacenet.com) | International coverage, CPC classification |
| [PQAI](https://projectpq.ai) | Plain-English semantic patent search |
| [PatentsView](https://patentsview.org) | USPTO data, assignee and inventor analysis |

**Search tip:** Use CPC (Cooperative Patent Classification) codes alongside keywords. Keywords change across decades and domains; structural classifications are more stable.

---

## Read the Background Section

Every patent's Background section describes the prior art — the landscape of solutions the inventor thought was insufficient. Reading Background sections of expired patents is often more valuable than reading the claims, because it records pain points that may still exist today.
